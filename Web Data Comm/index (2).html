02 — Course Summary

Key Concepts in Data Communication

A concise overview of the fundamental ideas explored throughout this course.

📡

Signals & Transmission

Data is carried as analogue or digital signals over a medium. Bandwidth, frequency, and amplitude determine how much information can be transmitted reliably over a given channel.

🔗

Transmission Media

Physical channels include twisted-pair copper wire, coaxial cable, and optical fibre. Wireless media such as radio waves, microwaves, and infrared light carry data without physical conductors.

🔀

Multiplexing

FDM, TDM, and WDM techniques allow multiple signals to share a single transmission medium simultaneously, dramatically increasing efficiency of communication links.

🛡️

Error Detection & Correction

Parity bits, CRC, and Hamming codes detect or correct bit errors introduced by noise, ensuring data integrity from sender to receiver across imperfect channels.

📦

Data Link & Flow Control

Protocols like HDLC and PPP manage framing, addressing, and flow control at Layer 2. Sliding window algorithms balance throughput against the risk of receiver buffer overflow.

🌐

Network Protocols & OSI Model

The seven-layer OSI reference model provides a universal framework for understanding how data travels from application logic down to electrical pulses on a wire and back again.

🔄

Circuit vs Packet Switching

Circuit switching dedicates a fixed path for the entire call (traditional telephony); packet switching breaks data into labelled packets routed independently — the backbone of the Internet.

🔐

Security in Data Communication

Encryption (symmetric/asymmetric), digital signatures, and secure protocols (TLS/SSL) safeguard data confidentiality and authenticity as it traverses public networks.

03 — Historical Context

The Electric Telegraph

The telegraph was the world's first electrical long-distance communication system, pioneering concepts that underpin all modern data networks.

The telegraph converted text into electrical pulses (Morse code) sent along copper wire strung between poles. The sender pressed a key to open/close a circuit; pulses travelled at near light-speed to a distant sounder where an operator decoded the clicks. This was the world's first practical digital communication system — binary in nature, just like modern data networks.
